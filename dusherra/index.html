Dussehra: Stories & Traditions

Explore the legends, celebrations, and deeper meaning of this vibrant festival.

Celebrations & Offerings

When is Dussehra Celebrated?

Dussehra, also known as Vijaya Dashami, is celebrated on the tenth day of the bright half of the Hindu calendar month. This is the month of Ashvin (also called Ashvayuja or Purattasi in some regions).

This day marks the end of the nine-night festival of Navaratri, and it usually falls in the month of September or October. It is a festival of victory that follows a period of worship and fasting.

Dussehra Celebrations Across India

North India

Marked by vibrant Ramleela performances and the grand burning of Ravana effigies, symbolizing the destruction of evil.

East India

Celebrated as Vijayadashami, the final day of Durga Puja, featuring grand processions for idol immersion and the joyous Sindoor Khela.

South India

Famous for the grandeur of Mysore Dasara and the tradition of arranging Golu dolls, representing deities and stories.

West India

The festival culminates in nine nights of vibrant Garba and Dandiya Raas dances. Garba is a traditional circular folk dance performed around a pot representing the Goddess, while Dandiya Raas is a lively performance with sticks, performed as a celebration of devotion and victory.

North Indian Tradition

During Sharad Navaratri in North India, many people observe fasts, consuming only phalahar, a diet of fruits, milk, and dry fruits. The offerings to the goddess are typically satvik (pure) foods such as kheer, halwa, poori, and kala chana (black chickpeas).

A significant ritual, especially on the eighth (Ashtami) or ninth (Navami) day, is Kanya Puja. Young girls are honored as manifestations of the Goddess and are offered a special meal of poori, sooji halwa, and kala chana as part of the ritual. While daily lentil offerings are not as prevalent as in South India, legumes like kala chana hold a special place in the rituals.

The Legends

Lord Rama's Victory

The evil ten-headed demon king, Ravana, had kidnapped Princess Sita. Lord Rama, with his brother Lakshmana and the brave monkey army led by Hanuman, fought a fierce battle. On the last day, a brave and noble Lord Rama shot an arrow that defeated Ravana, rescuing Sita and bringing light back to the world.

Lesson: The power of righteousness and courage always triumphs over arrogance and evil.

A Victory for Learning

In some parts of India, Vijaya Dashami is a day to celebrate knowledge itself. After honoring the Goddess of Knowledge, Saraswati, children begin their formal education by writing their very first letters on a slate or in a tray of rice. This tradition teaches us that learning is a powerful tool and a victory in itself.

Lesson: Knowledge is a form of victory, and learning is a celebration.

The Pandavas' Secret

During their exile, the brave Pandava brothers had to hide their powerful weapons in the branches of a Shami tree to stay hidden. When their exile ended on the day of Vijaya Dashami, they returned to the tree, retrieved their weapons, and prepared for the great war. This act showed their patience and courage, leading to their final victory.

Lesson: Perseverance and the fight for justice lead to ultimate victory.

Goddess Durga's Triumph

The powerful buffalo demon, Mahishasura, was creating chaos everywhere. All the gods combined their strength to create the fearless Goddess Durga. She rode on a lion and fought Mahishasura for nine long nights. On the tenth day, she finally defeated him, bringing peace and balance back to the universe.

Lesson: Divine strength and power (Shakti) can overcome tyranny and restore harmony.

The Story of Chanda & Munda

The Story

While the Devas (gods) were fleeing from the demon kings Shumbha and Nishumbha, they prayed to Goddess Durga. An incarnation of Durga, Goddess Ambika, emerged from the body of Parvati. Chanda and Munda, commanders of the demon army, saw Ambika and were overwhelmed by her beauty. They reported her radiance and grace to Shumbha, suggesting he should claim this "jewel among women."

Shumbha, driven by his ego, sent a messenger to demand that Durga become his wife. The goddess refused, stating she would only marry someone who could conquer her in battle. Angered, Shumbha sent his commanders, Chanda and Munda, to capture the goddess by force.

As the battle intensified, Chanda and Munda underestimated the goddess's power. But when she was confronted by them, she transformed into the fierce goddess Kali. With swift and decisive action, Kali beheaded both demons, earning her the name Chamunda.

Symbolic Meaning

  • Chanda and Munda: They represent blindness, flawed perception, and a lack of reasoning or understanding—a state of mind "without the head."
  • Defeat of Ignorance: Their defeat signifies how divine wisdom and truth triumph over negative qualities like arrogance, ego, and ignorance.

The Demon of Replication: Raktabija

The Story

After the defeat of Chanda and Munda, Shumbha and Nishumbha sent another fearsome commander, Raktabija. Raktabijasura, a powerful demon, gained a special boon that made him nearly invincible. Every single drop of blood that fell from his body onto the ground would instantly create a new, equally strong demon clone.

Durga's army and the seven divine mothers (Saptamatrikas) fought valiantly. They attacked Raktabija with their weapons, but as soon as his blood touched the earth, countless new demons emerged. The battlefield was quickly filled with an army of Raktabijas, overwhelming the goddesses. It seemed impossible to defeat him without the entire universe being overrun by his clones. To save the world, the mighty goddess Kali was summoned. With her dark form and fierce, powerful presence, she descended upon the battlefield, ready to confront the demon who could not be defeated by conventional means.

Symbolic Meaning

  • Raktabija: He represents the self-replicating nature of desires and negative qualities such as greed, jealousy, and anger. When we try to suppress them with force (like a weapon), they often multiply and become stronger.

The Seven Divine Mothers: Sapta Matrikas

Who Are They?

The Sapta Matrikas are a group of seven powerful Hindu goddesses, each a manifestation of the feminine energy (Shakti) of a major male deity. They emerged from the bodies of the gods to aid the supreme goddess Durga in her battle against the demon Raktabija.

  • Brahmani: The power of Brahma, associated with knowledge and creation.
  • Maheshvari: The power of Shiva, associated with protection and dissolution.
  • Kaumari: The power of Skanda (Kartikeya), associated with war and victory.
  • Vaishnavi: The power of Vishnu, associated with preservation and cosmic order.
  • Varahi: The power of Varaha (Vishnu's boar incarnation), associated with removing obstacles.
  • Indrani: The power of Indra, associated with courage and sovereignty.
  • Chamunda: A fierce manifestation of the Devi, associated with the slaying of demons.

Symbolic Meaning

  • The Collective Power: The Sapta Matrikas symbolize the truth that the one divine power, Shakti, can manifest in many different forms to defeat evil. The seven goddesses joining forces to fight Raktabija represents that all aspects of divine energy must work together to overcome complex challenges.

The Slaying of Raktabija

The Story

Seeing that conventional methods were failing, Goddess Durga called upon her ferocious form, Goddess Kali, to enter the battle. Durga and the Saptamatrikas would strike Raktabija, but before any of his blood could hit the ground, Kali would swiftly and completely drink it.

With this strategy, the demon’s blood never reached the earth, and no new clones were created. Finally, Raktabija was defeated, and the goddesses could proceed to face the final, most powerful demons.

Symbolic Meaning

  • The Solution: This story teaches that to defeat a deeply rooted problem or negative habit, we must address the source. Instead of simply fighting the external symptoms, we must contain and eliminate the root cause.

The Final Battle: Shumbha & Nishumbha

The Story

The demon brothers, Shumbha and Nishumbha, represented the ultimate forms of ego and ignorance. Having defeated all the gods, they believed they were invincible. The goddesses, after defeating Raktabija, faced them directly. The final battle was a fierce and powerful clash, with Durga leading the charge.

Through a combination of divine power and strategy, Durga and her allies defeated the arrogant brothers, restoring cosmic order and ending the reign of terror. This final victory is celebrated on Vijayadashami, symbolizing the ultimate triumph of good over evil.

Symbolic Meaning

  • The Final Ego: Shumbha and Nishumbha represent the last, most stubborn aspects of our ego and pride that must be overcome to achieve true spiritual liberation. Their defeat marks the culmination of the nine-night battle against internal and external darkness.
